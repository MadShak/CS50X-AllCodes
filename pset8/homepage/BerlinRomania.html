Hello World, Im Cioran...

Berlin n Romania

In 1933, he won a scholarship to the University of Berlin, where he had contact with Nicolai Hartmann and Ludwig Klages. In Berlin, he is interested in the actions of the Nazi regime, and collaborates with a column by Vremea addressing the subject (then Cioran confesses: "there is currently no politician that arouses greater sympathy and admiration than Hitler, "[1] and praises the Night of the Long Knives -" what would humanity have to lose if the lives of some imbeciles were suppressed? "), and, in a letter addressed to Petru Comarnescu, describes himself as a" Hitler ". Cioran had a similar view on Italian fascism. He saluted the victories of the Second Italo-Abisínia War, that: "Fascism is a shock, without which Italy is a compromise compared to Romania today". The first book de Cioran, Sur les cimes du désespoir (At the height of despair, translated more precisely as: "In the summits of Despair ") was published in Romania in 1934 and awarded the Commissions Prize and the Young Writers Prize as being one of the best books written by an unprecedented young author. They followed: The Book of Disillusions (1935), The Transfiguration of Romania (1936) and Lágrimas e Santos (1937), also published in Romania. Although it was not effective member in Romania, Cioran began to take an interest in the ideas propagated by Iron Guard, a ultra-right organization, whose nationalist ideology he supported until the beginning of World War II, despite supposedly disapproving of his violent methods. Later he denies that movement which he approached in youth, criticizing right-wing and fascist movements as being passions and diseases of youth.
